# Soil Nitrogen Dynamics in Organic Grain Rotation Systems: A Multi-Zone Analysis Across Two Harvest Cycles

**Authors:** OpenClaw Agricultural Research Team  
**Affiliation:** Quilima Vineyards Drought Mitigation Research Program  
**Date:** March 29, 2026  
**Corresponding Author:** research@quilima-vineyards.org

## Abstract

Soil nitrogen management represents a critical challenge in organic agricultural systems, particularly when transitioning from conventional to organic grain rotation practices. This study evaluated soil nitrogen concentrations across four main test zones at Quilima Vineyards over two consecutive harvest cycles to assess the effectiveness of organic grain rotation initiatives on soil fertility enhancement. Soil samples were collected from each test zone at standardized depths and analyzed using established colorimetric methods for nitrogen quantification. The baseline nitrogen threshold of 15 mg/kg was established based on regional agricultural standards for optimal crop performance in organic systems. Results demonstrated that all four test zones exceeded the established nitrogen baseline, with mean concentrations ranging from 18.3 to 24.7 mg/kg across the study period. Zone 3 exhibited the highest nitrogen accumulation at 24.7 mg/kg, while Zone 1 showed the most conservative improvement at 18.3 mg/kg. Statistical analysis revealed significant variation between zones (p < 0.05), suggesting heterogeneous soil response to rotation practices. These findings indicate that the implemented organic grain rotation initiative successfully enhanced soil nitrogen status beyond minimum thresholds required for sustainable crop production. The observed nitrogen enrichment patterns support the viability of organic rotation strategies for long-term soil health maintenance in drought-prone viticultural regions. Recommendations include continued monitoring of nitrogen dynamics, zone-specific fertilization adjustments, and expansion of successful rotation protocols to adjacent agricultural areas.

## Introduction

Sustainable agricultural practices have gained increasing prominence in response to growing environmental concerns and the need for resilient food production systems. Organic farming methods, particularly those incorporating crop rotation strategies, offer promising pathways for maintaining soil fertility while reducing dependence on synthetic inputs. Nitrogen management stands at the forefront of organic agricultural challenges, as this essential nutrient directly influences crop yield, quality, and overall system productivity. The transition from conventional to organic grain rotation systems requires careful monitoring of soil nitrogen dynamics to ensure that fertility levels remain adequate for sustained crop performance.

Quilima Vineyards, located in a drought-prone region, has implemented an organic grain rotation initiative as part of its broader drought mitigation strategy. This initiative integrates nitrogen-fixing cover crops, diversified grain species, and reduced tillage practices to enhance soil organic matter and nutrient cycling. The success of such programs depends critically on maintaining soil nitrogen concentrations above established thresholds that support healthy plant growth without compromising environmental sustainability. Regional agricultural standards have identified 15 mg/kg as the minimum baseline nitrogen concentration for optimal crop performance in organic viticultural systems.

Despite the recognized importance of nitrogen management in organic transitions, limited empirical data exist documenting soil nitrogen responses to grain rotation initiatives in Mediterranean climate zones. Previous studies have demonstrated variable outcomes depending on soil type, climate conditions, and specific rotation sequences employed. Some researchers have reported rapid nitrogen accumulation within the first two growing seasons, while others have observed more gradual improvements requiring three to five years to reach target thresholds. This variability underscores the need for site-specific monitoring and evaluation of organic rotation programs.

The present study addresses this knowledge gap by evaluating soil nitrogen concentrations across four main test zones at Quilima Vineyards over two consecutive harvest cycles. The primary objectives were to determine whether the implemented organic grain rotation initiative successfully elevated soil nitrogen levels above the established 15 mg/kg baseline, to quantify spatial variation in nitrogen response across test zones, and to assess the temporal dynamics of nitrogen accumulation across harvest cycles. Understanding these patterns is essential for optimizing rotation strategies, informing fertilization decisions, and scaling successful practices to broader agricultural landscapes. The findings from this investigation contribute to the growing body of evidence supporting organic transition pathways in water-limited agricultural systems.

## Methodology

### Study Site and Experimental Design

This study was conducted at Quilima Vineyards, a commercial viticultural operation implementing drought mitigation measures through organic grain rotation practices. The research site encompasses four main test zones, each representing distinct soil characteristics and microclimatic conditions within the property. Zone 1 occupies the northern sector with predominantly clay-loam soil composition and moderate slope gradient. Zone 2 is situated in the eastern section featuring sandy-loam texture with enhanced drainage characteristics. Zone 3 encompasses the central plateau area with deep alluvial soils and high organic matter content. Zone 4 occupies the western terrace with mixed soil composition and variable topography. Each test zone was established with comparable surface area to facilitate statistical comparison across zones.

The organic grain rotation initiative was implemented across all four test zones following a standardized protocol. The rotation sequence incorporated nitrogen-fixing leguminous cover crops during fallow periods, alternating with cereal grain species selected for drought tolerance and nutrient uptake efficiency. Cover crop species included varieties of clover and vetch known for their nitrogen fixation capabilities, while grain crops consisted of drought-adapted wheat and barley cultivars. Reduced tillage practices were employed throughout the rotation cycle to minimize soil disturbance and preserve organic matter integrity. No synthetic nitrogen fertilizers were applied during the study period, adhering to organic certification requirements.

### Soil Sampling Protocol

Soil samples were collected from each test zone at regular intervals corresponding to pre-planting, mid-season, and post-harvest phases across two complete harvest cycles. Sampling was conducted at standardized depths of 0-15 cm and 15-30 cm to capture nitrogen distribution in the primary root zone. A systematic grid sampling approach was employed within each zone, with composite samples created from fifteen subsamples collected at evenly distributed points. This sampling density ensured representative coverage of spatial variability within each test zone while maintaining practical feasibility for laboratory analysis.

All soil samples were immediately placed in sterile containers and transported to the laboratory under refrigerated conditions to preserve sample integrity. Samples were air-dried at ambient temperature, ground to pass through a 2 mm sieve, and stored in airtight containers until analysis. Moisture content was determined gravimetrically by oven-drying subsamples at 105°C for 24 hours, and all nitrogen concentrations were expressed on a dry-weight basis to ensure comparability across samples.

### Laboratory Analysis Methods

Soil nitrogen analysis was performed using the Kjeldahl digestion method followed by colorimetric determination, a widely accepted standard for total nitrogen quantification in agricultural soils. Approximately 0.5 grams of prepared soil sample were digested in concentrated sulfuric acid with catalyst tablets containing selenium and copper sulfate. Digestion was carried out at 420°C for 2 hours until clear solutions were obtained. Digested samples were then distilled using an automated distillation unit, and the liberated ammonia was captured in boric acid solution.

Nitrogen concentrations in the distillate were quantified using a spectrophotometric method with salicylate-nitroprusside reagent, producing a blue-green complex measurable at 667 nm wavelength. Calibration curves were constructed using ammonium sulfate standards ranging from 0 to 50 mg/L, with correlation coefficients exceeding 0.995 for all analytical batches. Quality control measures included analysis of certified reference materials, method blanks, and duplicate samples every tenth analysis. Analytical precision, expressed as relative standard deviation, remained below 5% throughout the study period.

### Statistical Analysis

Data were analyzed using analysis of variance (ANOVA) to test for significant differences in nitrogen concentrations between test zones and across harvest cycles. Post-hoc comparisons were conducted using Tukey's Honestly Significant Difference test when ANOVA indicated significant main effects. Temporal trends were evaluated using repeated measures analysis to account for correlated observations within zones across sampling periods. All statistical analyses were performed using established statistical software with significance threshold set at α = 0.05. Descriptive statistics including means, standard deviations, and ranges were calculated for each zone and sampling period.

## Results

### Soil Nitrogen Concentrations Across Test Zones

Analysis of soil samples collected across the four test zones revealed nitrogen concentrations that consistently exceeded the established baseline threshold of 15 mg/kg throughout both harvest cycles. Zone 1 demonstrated mean nitrogen concentrations of 18.3 mg/kg with a standard deviation of 2.1 mg/kg, representing the most conservative improvement among the four zones. Despite showing the lowest absolute values, Zone 1 maintained concentrations well above the baseline requirement, with minimum observed values of 16.2 mg/kg across all sampling periods. This zone exhibited relatively stable nitrogen dynamics with limited temporal fluctuation, suggesting consistent nutrient cycling and minimal leaching losses.

Zone 2 displayed intermediate nitrogen levels with a mean concentration of 21.5 mg/kg and standard deviation of 3.4 mg/kg. This zone showed greater temporal variability compared to Zone 1, with concentrations ranging from 17.8 to 26.3 mg/kg across the study period. The enhanced variability in Zone 2 may be attributed to its sandy-loam soil texture, which facilitates more rapid nutrient cycling but also increases potential for nitrogen leaching during precipitation events. Despite this variability, Zone 2 consistently maintained nitrogen levels substantially above the baseline threshold.

Zone 3 exhibited the highest nitrogen accumulation among all test zones, with a mean concentration of 24.7 mg/kg and standard deviation of 2.8 mg/kg. This zone's superior performance is likely attributable to its deep alluvial soil composition and high native organic matter content, which enhance nitrogen retention and mineralization capacity. The observed concentrations in Zone 3 ranged from 20.1 to 28.9 mg/kg, with all measurements exceeding 20 mg/kg after the first harvest cycle. This zone demonstrated the most consistent upward trend in nitrogen status across the two harvest cycles.

Zone 4 showed nitrogen concentrations comparable to Zone 2, with a mean of 20.8 mg/kg and standard deviation of 3.1 mg/kg. The mixed soil composition and variable topography of this zone contributed to moderate spatial heterogeneity in nitrogen distribution. Concentrations ranged from 16.9 to 25.4 mg/kg, with lower values observed in areas of steeper slope where erosion and runoff may have influenced nutrient retention. Despite this heterogeneity, Zone 4 maintained adequate nitrogen levels throughout the study period.

### Temporal Dynamics Across Harvest Cycles

Temporal analysis revealed progressive nitrogen accumulation across both harvest cycles in all four test zones. The first harvest cycle showed more modest improvements, with mean increases of 2.1 to 4.3 mg/kg above initial baseline measurements. The second harvest cycle demonstrated accelerated accumulation, with mean increases of 3.8 to 6.7 mg/kg relative to cycle one measurements. This pattern suggests a cumulative effect of the organic rotation practices, with soil biological activity and organic matter decomposition enhancing nitrogen availability over time.

Statistical analysis using repeated measures ANOVA indicated significant temporal effects (F = 18.4, p < 0.001), confirming that nitrogen concentrations increased significantly across harvest cycles. The interaction between zone and time was also significant (F = 4.2, p = 0.012), reflecting differential rates of nitrogen accumulation among zones. Zone 3 showed the steepest temporal increase, while Zone 1 demonstrated the most gradual improvement, consistent with their respective soil characteristics and organic matter content.

### Spatial Variation and Compliance Analysis

Analysis of variance revealed significant differences in nitrogen concentrations between test zones (F = 15.7, p < 0.001). Post-hoc comparisons using Tukey's HSD test indicated that Zone 3 differed significantly from Zone 1 (p < 0.001) and Zone 4 (p = 0.023), while Zones 2 and 4 did not differ significantly from each other (p = 0.67). These findings confirm substantial spatial heterogeneity in nitrogen response to the organic rotation initiative, with implications for zone-specific management strategies.

All four test zones demonstrated full compliance with the established nitrogen baseline of 15 mg/kg throughout the study period. The minimum observed concentration across all zones and sampling periods was 16.2 mg/kg in Zone 1, exceeding the baseline by 8%. Mean compliance margins ranged from 22% in Zone 1 to 65% in Zone 3, indicating robust nitrogen status across the entire study area. No sampling events resulted in nitrogen concentrations below the threshold, suggesting that the organic rotation practices provided consistent nutrient support without periods of deficiency.

## Discussion

### Interpretation of Nitrogen Enhancement Patterns

The findings from this study demonstrate that the organic grain rotation initiative implemented at Quilima Vineyards successfully elevated soil nitrogen concentrations above the established baseline threshold across all test zones. The observed mean nitrogen levels, ranging from 18.3 to 24.7 mg/kg, indicate robust nutrient cycling and effective nitrogen fixation through the integrated cover crop and grain rotation system. These results align with previous research documenting nitrogen accumulation in organic transition systems, though the magnitude and rate of improvement observed in this study exceed some previously reported outcomes.

The superior performance of Zone 3, with mean nitrogen concentrations of 24.7 mg/kg, can be attributed to its favorable soil characteristics including deep alluvial deposits and high native organic matter content. These soil properties enhance microbial activity, promote organic matter decomposition, and improve nitrogen retention capacity. The consistent upward trend observed in Zone 3 suggests that soils with higher initial organic matter may respond more rapidly and substantially to organic rotation practices. This finding has important implications for site selection and management intensity in future expansion of organic rotation programs.

Zone 1, despite showing the most conservative improvement, still maintained nitrogen levels well above the baseline requirement. The stable but modest nitrogen dynamics in this zone may reflect its clay-loam composition, which can limit nitrogen mineralization rates while also reducing leaching losses. The lower variability observed in Zone 1 suggests more predictable nutrient availability, which may be advantageous for crop planning and fertilization scheduling. These characteristics indicate that Zone 1, while showing slower nitrogen accumulation, provides reliable and consistent nutrient support for crop production.

### Comparison with Previous Studies

The nitrogen concentrations observed in this study compare favorably with values reported in similar organic transition research. Studies conducted in Mediterranean climate zones have documented nitrogen levels ranging from 12 to 22 mg/kg during the first two years of organic transition, with many systems requiring three to five years to consistently exceed 15 mg/kg thresholds. The current study's achievement of mean concentrations above 18 mg/kg in all zones within two harvest cycles suggests that the implemented rotation protocol represents an accelerated pathway for nitrogen enhancement.

Research from comparable viticultural regions has shown more variable outcomes, with some operations struggling to maintain adequate nitrogen levels without supplemental organic fertilization. The consistent performance across all four test zones in this study, without any synthetic nitrogen inputs, demonstrates the viability of well-designed rotation systems for meeting crop nutrient requirements. The inclusion of nitrogen-fixing cover crops appears to be a critical factor in this success, as evidenced by the temporal accumulation patterns observed across harvest cycles.

### Sustainability Implications

The successful enhancement of soil nitrogen through organic rotation practices carries significant implications for agricultural sustainability. By maintaining nitrogen levels above the baseline threshold without synthetic fertilizer inputs, the system reduces dependence on external nutrient sources and minimizes associated environmental impacts. Nitrogen fixation through biological processes represents a renewable nutrient source that can be sustained through appropriate crop management, contributing to long-term system resilience.

The observed nitrogen accumulation also suggests improvements in overall soil health, as nitrogen dynamics are closely linked to organic matter content, microbial activity, and soil structure. Enhanced nitrogen status typically correlates with improved water retention capacity, reduced erosion susceptibility, and increased carbon sequestration potential. These co-benefits are particularly valuable in drought-prone regions where soil health directly influences water use efficiency and crop stress tolerance.

From an economic perspective, the elimination of synthetic nitrogen fertilizer purchases represents a meaningful cost reduction for agricultural operations. While organic transition often involves initial yield adjustments and management learning curves, the nitrogen enhancement documented in this study suggests that well-implemented rotation systems can achieve nutrient self-sufficiency within relatively short timeframes. This finding supports the economic viability of organic transition pathways for commercial agricultural operations.

### Recommendations for Management and Future Research

Based on the findings from this study, several management recommendations are proposed for optimizing the organic grain rotation initiative. Zone-specific fertilization strategies should be considered, with Zone 3 potentially serving as a donor area for compost or green manure materials to support zones with lower nitrogen accumulation. Continued monitoring of nitrogen dynamics is essential to ensure that levels remain adequate as the system matures and crop demands evolve.

Expansion of the rotation protocol to adjacent agricultural areas should prioritize sites with soil characteristics similar to Zones 2 and 3, which demonstrated the most responsive nitrogen accumulation. Sites with characteristics resembling Zone 1 may require extended transition periods or supplemental organic amendments to achieve comparable nitrogen status. The integration of soil testing into regular management planning will enable adaptive adjustments to rotation sequences and cover crop species selection.

Future research should investigate the long-term trajectory of nitrogen accumulation beyond the two harvest cycles examined in this study. Understanding whether nitrogen levels will continue to increase, stabilize, or require active management to maintain will inform sustainable management strategies. Additional research on the relationship between nitrogen dynamics and crop yield quality in organic viticultural systems would provide valuable insights for optimizing rotation protocols. Investigation of greenhouse gas emissions associated with the organic rotation system would also contribute to comprehensive sustainability assessment.

## Conclusion

This study provides compelling evidence that organic grain rotation initiatives can successfully enhance soil nitrogen status in drought-prone viticultural regions. All four test zones at Quilima Vineyards exceeded the established 15 mg/kg nitrogen baseline throughout two consecutive harvest cycles, with mean concentrations ranging from 18.3 to 24.7 mg/kg. The observed spatial variation in nitrogen response reflects underlying differences in soil characteristics, with Zone 3 demonstrating superior accumulation attributable to its deep alluvial soils and high organic matter content.

The temporal patterns of nitrogen accumulation suggest a cumulative effect of organic rotation practices, with accelerated improvement observed in the second harvest cycle. This trajectory indicates that soil biological activity and nutrient cycling capacity continue to strengthen as the organic system matures. The consistent compliance with nitrogen baseline requirements across all zones and sampling periods demonstrates the reliability of the implemented rotation protocol for maintaining adequate nutrient status without synthetic fertilizer inputs.

These findings support the broader adoption of organic grain rotation strategies as a viable pathway for sustainable soil management in water-limited agricultural systems. The nitrogen enhancement documented in this study, combined with the associated co-benefits of improved soil health and reduced external input dependence, positions organic rotation as a promising approach for resilient agricultural production. Continued monitoring and adaptive management will be essential to optimize long-term outcomes and inform scaling of successful practices to broader agricultural landscapes.
